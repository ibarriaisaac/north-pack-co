Best Three-Season Backpacking Tent: REI Co-op Half Dome 2 Plus

The REI Co-op Half Dome 2 Plus REI Co-op Half Dome 2 Plus hits a sweet spot that most backpackers are looking for — reasonable weight, a livable interior, and solid weather protection without a premium price tag. The "Plus" in the name refers to the extra width, which makes a real difference if you're sharing the tent or just want room to spread out gear.

Specs worth noting: the tent weighs around 4 lbs 13 oz (packaged), includes two doors and two vestibules, and uses a DAC Featherlight pole system that's both lightweight and durable. The fly coverage is robust enough to handle unexpected rain, and the color-coded clip system means setup takes about 10 minutes on your first try.

Who it's for: Backpackers who want a trustworthy tent for spring through fall use without spending $500+. It's not ultralight, but it's the kind of tent that lasts years with basic care.

Watch out for: It's heavier than ultralight options. If you're counting every ounce, look at the Big Agnes below.

Best Ultralight Backpacking Tent: Big Agnes Copper Spur HV UL2

If weight is your primary concern, the Big Agnes Copper Spur HV UL2 Big Agnes Copper Spur HV UL2 is one of the most refined ultralight tents on the market. Coming in at just over 2 lbs for the ultralight version, it packs down small and still delivers a genuinely comfortable interior thanks to the "High Volume" hub design that creates near-vertical walls.

It uses silicone-nylon fabric for both the fly and floor, which is lighter than standard polyester but requires slightly more careful handling. Two doors, two vestibules, and plenty of interior pockets keep things organized on multi-day trips. The hub-and-pole architecture goes up fast once you're familiar with it.

Who it's for: Gram-conscious backpackers, thru-hikers, and anyone who wants a tent they'll barely notice in their pack but won't compromise on livability.

Watch out for: The silnylon fabric doesn't handle abrasion as well as heavier alternatives. Use a footprint and be mindful of rocky campsites.

Best Four-Season / Winter Tent: MSR Access 3

Winter camping and alpine climbing demand a fundamentally different shelter. The MSR Access 3 MSR Access 3 is built to handle snow loading, sustained high winds, and temperature extremes that would destroy a standard three-season tent. It uses a hybrid design that keeps ventilation better than a fully sealed expedition tent, making it more comfortable during shoulder-season use while still being bombproof in real winter conditions.

The Access 3 sleeps three, which is useful for groups sharing warmth, or two with extra gear storage. The asymmetric, three-pole architecture sheds snow efficiently, and the reinforced seams and heavy-duty zippers are built to last through repeated winter deployments. At around 5 lbs 8 oz, it's remarkably light for a true four-season shelter.

Who it's for: Mountaineers, winter backpackers, and anyone venturing into conditions where a collapsed tent could become a survival situation.

Watch out for: The price is significant. If you're only camping in mild three-season conditions, this is more tent than you need.

Best Budget Car Camping Tent: Coleman Sundome 4

Car camping doesn't require spending $400. The Coleman Sundome 4 Coleman Sundome 4 is proof that a straightforward, well-built tent can cost less than $120 and still perform reliably across years of weekend use. It sleeps four people comfortably, pitches in about 15 minutes, and handles moderate rain well thanks to its WeatherTec system with welded floors and inverted seams.

The freestanding dome design is stable in light to moderate wind, and the large windows and ground vent help prevent the condensation buildup that plagues cheaper tents. It's not the tent you bring on a backpacking trip, but it doesn't need to be.

Who it's for: Families, festival-goers, casual weekend campers, and anyone who needs a reliable tent without a serious financial commitment.

Watch out for: This isn't a storm tent. In heavy sustained rain or wind, you'll want something more substantial.

Best Family Car Camping Tent: Nemo Wagontop 6P

When you need genuine living space — standing room, separate sleeping areas, room for gear — the Nemo Wagontop 6P Nemo Wagontop 6P delivers. Its unique curved pole architecture creates a barn-shaped interior with a 6'6" peak height, so adults can actually move around without crouching. The interior divider creates two separate rooms, which matters enormously when you're camping with kids for a week.

The Wagontop uses a full-coverage fly that does an excellent job of keeping water out in serious rain. Setup takes two people and about 20 minutes, but the result is a genuinely comfortable shelter that holds up trip after trip. Ventilation panels on the fly prevent the greenhouse effect on warm nights.

Who it's for: Families with children, groups of adults who want personal space, and anyone treating car camping as a comfortable outdoor retreat rather than a survival exercise.

Best Solo Expedition Tent: Hilleberg Soulo

The Hilleberg Soulo Hilleberg Soulo is in a different category than almost everything else on this list. Hilleberg tents are hand-built in Sweden using proprietary Kerlon fabric — a laminated ripstop that is dramatically stronger than standard tent materials at a comparable weight. The Soulo is a single-person tunnel tent designed for serious expeditions where failure is not an option.

It pitches in under three minutes using the connected inner/outer design that lets you set it up without the inner getting wet. It withstands extreme winds and snow loads. The interior is compact but well-designed, with smart storage pockets and a vestibule large enough for a full pack and wet gear.

Who it's for: Solo alpinists, polar travelers, and experienced campers who want the most reliable tent they can buy and are willing to pay for that confidence.

Watch out for: The price is hard to swallow. For most people, it's more tent than their conditions require. But for those who need it, nothing compares.

The Sea to Summit Telos TR2 Sea to Summit Telos TR2 stands out for one key design feature: it pitches with the fly first, keeping the inner dry during setup in rain. The tensioned ridge architecture creates excellent interior volume for a two-person tent, and the cross-ventilation system is one of the most effective on the market for managing condensation.

Buying Advice

Match the tent to your actual conditions. A three-season tent is the right choice for 90% of campers. A four-season tent is heavier, more expensive, and unnecessary unless you're genuinely camping in snow or high winds. Be honest about where and when you camp.

Weight matters most when you carry the tent. For car camping, ignore the weight spec. For backpacking, it becomes central to your decision.

Check the hydrostatic head rating. A floor rating above 3,000mm and a fly rating above 1,500mm handles most conditions well. Cheaper tents cut corners here first.

Don't skip the footprint. A tent footprint extends the life of your floor significantly and costs a fraction of a replacement tent.

Buy the size up if you can afford it. A "two-person" tent is technically accurate but rarely comfortable for two adults with gear. If your budget allows, size up.
