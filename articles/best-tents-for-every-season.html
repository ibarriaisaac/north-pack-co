Best Budget 3-Season Tent: REI Co-op Half Dome 2 Plus

The REI Co-op Half Dome 2 Plus REI Co-op Half Dome 2 Plus is one of the most popular entry-level tents on the market for good reason. It handles three seasons reliably, sets up quickly with color-coded clips, and offers more interior space than most tents in its price range — the "Plus" designation means the floor is noticeably wider than the standard Half Dome 2.

Key specs:

  • Weight: 5 lbs 6 oz
  • Floor area: 37.5 sq ft
  • Seasons: 3

    • The rainfly coverage is solid for spring and summer storms, though it won't hold up in serious wind. The aluminum poles are durable enough for regular use. If you're new to backpacking or camping a few times a year and don't want to spend over $250, this is where to start.

    What we'd change: The vestibule is smaller than competitors at this price point, so gear storage can get tight in heavy rain.

    Best Ultralight 3-Season Tent: MSR Hubba Hubba NX 2

    The MSR Hubba Hubba NX 2 MSR Hubba Hubba NX 2 has earned its reputation through years of refinement. It's the go-to choice for serious backpackers who want a freestanding tent that sheds weight without sacrificing livability or weather protection.

    Key specs:

  • Weight: 3 lbs 9 oz
  • Floor area: 29 sq ft
  • Seasons: 3

    • The dual-door, dual-vestibule design is a major practical advantage — both occupants get their own entrance and gear storage area. The mesh inner keeps things breathable in summer, while the full rainfly provides genuine rain protection. MSR's Easton Syclone poles are stiff and light, and the whole thing freestandingly pitches in under five minutes once you've done it a couple of times.

    The Hubba Hubba is pricier than the REI Half Dome, but you're paying for roughly two fewer pounds on your back and noticeably higher build quality.

    Best All-Around Ultralight Tent: Big Agnes Copper Spur HV UL2

    The Big Agnes Copper Spur HV UL2 Big Agnes Copper Spur HV UL2 consistently lands at the top of backpacker gear lists, and it's well deserved. The "HV" stands for high volume — the nearly vertical walls give this tent a remarkably roomy feel for something that weighs under three pounds.

    Key specs:

  • Weight: 2 lbs 14 oz
  • Floor area: 29 sq ft
  • Seasons: 3

    • What separates this from the Hubba Hubba is the weight-to-livability ratio. The Copper Spur uses a single-pole hubbed design that creates more headroom without extra ounces. There are four interior pockets, two gear lofts, and a media pocket — small touches that matter on longer trips. The downside is that the floor and fly materials are thinner than competitors, so you'll want to use a footprint on rocky terrain and treat the tent with care.

    For thru-hikers, weekend warriors, or anyone who walks long miles and sleeps light, this is the tent to beat.

    Best Tent for Winter Backpacking: MSR Access 3

    The MSR Access 3 MSR Access 3 is a four-season tent built specifically for ski touring, snowshoeing, and winter backpacking where conditions can turn severe but you still need to move efficiently and keep weight manageable.

    Key specs:

  • Weight: 5 lbs 3 oz
  • Floor area: 38.5 sq ft
  • Seasons: 4

    • The Access differs from typical mountaineering tents by prioritizing livability alongside storm protection. The hybrid mesh-and-fabric inner keeps things warmer than a pure mesh tent, while the fly's low profile and strong pole structure handle wind and snow loading. The side-entry doors make it easier to get in and out of with boots and crampons.

    This isn't the tent for true technical mountaineering — for that, see the Eldorado below. But for anyone spending nights out in winter snow without vertical objectives, the Access 3 is a smarter and more comfortable choice.

    Best Mountaineering Tent: Black Diamond Eldorado

    The Black Diamond Eldorado Black Diamond Eldorado is built for climbers who need a shelter that stays standing in conditions that would destroy a three-season tent. It's a genuine four-season mountaineering shelter designed for high camps, exposed ridgelines, and sustained severe weather.

    Key specs:

  • Weight: 5 lbs 5 oz
  • Floor area: 26.5 sq ft
  • Seasons: 4

    • The dome geometry and DAC Featherlight NFL poles create exceptional structural integrity. The fly reaches nearly to the ground on all sides, and the doors are reinforced against wind. Interior space is spartan — this is a survival shelter, not a glamping tent — but everything is designed with purpose. The Eldorado is not a casual purchase. If you're car camping or doing standard backpacking, this is overkill. But if you're heading to altitude or into expedition conditions, don't cut corners on your shelter.

    Key specs:

Buying Advice: How to Choose the Right Tent

Match the tent to your actual conditions, not your aspirations. A four-season mountaineering tent is miserable on a warm summer camping trip, and a three-season backpacking tent can become dangerous in winter. Be honest about where and when you'll be camping.

Pay attention to packed weight versus livability. If you're backpacking, every ounce matters. If you're car camping, prioritize space and comfort over weight savings.

Vestibule size matters more than people think. Gear storage outside the sleeping area keeps condensation and clutter manageable. Look for at least 10–12 sq ft of vestibule space per person in a backpacking tent.

Freestanding versus non-freestanding: Freestanding tents are easier to pitch and reposition. Non-freestanding trekking pole tents save weight but require stakes and practice. Know which setup style you'll actually use correctly.

Set it up at home first. Regardless of which tent you buy, pitch it in your backyard before your first trip. Pole damage, missing stakes, and setup confusion are best discovered before you're wet and exhausted.
