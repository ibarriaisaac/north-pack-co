Best 40L Travel Backpacks

A 40-liter backpack hits a practical sweet spot: large enough for a week-long trip, small enough to clear most carry-on size restrictions. Whether you're hopping between hostels, heading into the backcountry, or navigating airport terminals, the right 40L pack will keep you organized without slowing you down. This guide covers the best options across different travel styles, with honest assessments of what each bag does well and where it falls short.

Osprey Farpoint 40

The Osprey Farpoint 40 Osprey Farpoint 40 remains one of the most recommended travel packs on the market for good reason. It opens like a suitcase from the front panel, making it straightforward to pack and access items without digging. The harness and shoulder straps zip away behind a dedicated panel, keeping them protected during checked or overhead bin storage and giving the bag a cleaner profile when you don't need them.

What works well: The suspension system is surprisingly capable for a travel-focused bag. You can carry this comfortably for hours in transit. The separate laptop sleeve in the back panel adds organization without crowding the main compartment. At 1.59 kg, it's not the lightest option here, but it's sturdy. What to watch: The hip belt is functional rather than load-bearing in any serious sense. For longer hikes with a heavier load, you'll feel the weight in your shoulders. It's also not as feature-rich internally as some competitors—organization depends heavily on what you pack it with. Best for: First-time travel backpackers or anyone who wants a proven, reliable bag without overthinking the purchase.

Nomatic Travel Pack 40L

If you travel with a laptop, tablets, external batteries, and cables, the Nomatic Travel Pack 40L Nomatic Travel Pack 40L was designed around your habits. The internal organization is genuinely impressive: dedicated slots for cords, a water bottle pocket, a fleece-lined sunglasses case, a shoe compartment, and a magnetic water-resistant zipper on the main compartment. The exterior is made from ballistic nylon with water-resistant zippers throughout.

What works well: The clamshell opening and systematic layout reduce the usual scramble for buried items. TSA-friendly laptop access is smooth, and the bag passes most airline carry-on gauges without issue. For urban travel and business trips, it's hard to match. What to watch: At around 1.8 kg empty, it's one of the heavier bags on this list before you've packed a single item. The structure that makes it so organized also makes it rigid—it won't compress easily into a tight overhead bin. The price point is also at the high end, so this isn't the right call for casual or infrequent travelers. Best for: Frequent flyers who travel light on clothing but heavy on tech gear.

Gregory Facet 45

Technically sitting slightly above 40L, the Gregory Facet 45 Gregory Facet 45 earns its place here because it's one of the best crossover options for travelers who also want to hit the trail. Gregory built this with their FreeFloat Dynamic Suspension, which lets the hipbelt move with your stride rather than staying rigidly attached. The result is a bag that transfers load efficiently on uneven terrain while remaining manageable in urban settings.

What works well: Ventilated back panel, load lifter straps, and a removable top lid make this feel like a proper hiking pack. The frame sheet can be removed to reduce bulk and weight if you're moving between city and trail. Compression straps help keep the profile slim when you're not fully loaded. What to watch: It's not as carry-on friendly as some competitors due to external attachment points and a more traditional outdoor silhouette. If airport efficiency is your top priority, you may prefer a more travel-specific design. Best for: Adventure travelers who spend time on both trails and in transit and don't want to carry two separate bags.

Tortuga Setout 45

The Tortuga Setout 45 Tortuga Setout 45 was built specifically for long travel days, and the ergonomics show it. The padded back panel has a suspended mesh design to reduce heat buildup, and the shoulder straps are thick enough to handle weight distribution over extended carries. Like the Farpoint, it opens fully from the front panel, giving suitcase-style access to the main compartment.

What works well: Tortuga put real effort into the internal layout. A lockable main zipper, a laptop sleeve that holds up to 17-inch screens, a dedicated organization panel, and a water bottle pocket on each side cover most practical needs. The bag also compresses well when partially full, which helps with varied packing loads across a long trip. What to watch: The Setout runs slightly large for smaller-framed travelers—the torso length may not suit those under 5'4" without adjustment. Some users also note the side water bottle pockets are tight for larger bottles. Best for: Long-haul travelers who spend significant time on their feet between destinations.

Peak Design Travel Backpack 45L

The Peak Design Travel Backpack 45L Peak Design Travel Backpack 45L is the most versatile bag on this list if you're willing to invest in the ecosystem. The MagLatch closure on the top expands or contracts the bag between roughly 30L and 45L depending on how much you're carrying. The weatherproof 400D nylon shell is tougher than it looks, and the clean exterior hides what is a genuinely clever internal organization system.

What works well: The bag is modular. Peak Design sells internal packing cubes and camera inserts designed specifically for its internal dimensions. The result is a bag that can function as a camera bag, a laptop bag, or a full travel pack depending on what inserts you drop in. Side access zippers open directly into the main compartment without unpacking everything. What to watch: Getting the most out of this bag means spending on the additional inserts, which adds to an already premium price. Without the cubes, the main compartment is one large open space that requires self-discipline to keep organized. Best for: Photographers, content creators, or minimalist travelers who want to maximize flexibility with a single bag.

REI Co-op Ruckpack 40

For buyers who want a capable 40L bag without committing to a premium price, the REI Co-op Ruckpack 40 REI Co-op Ruckpack 40 punches well above its cost. It's built for adventure travel rather than pure urban commuting, with a beefy external frame, attachment loops for trekking poles, and a removable rain cover included in the price.

What works well: The organization is practical and well-thought-out for outdoor use. Multiple zippered pockets, a sleeping bag compartment with a removable divider, and a hydration sleeve make this a solid companion for camping and hiking trips. REI's build quality control at this price range is consistently good. What to watch: It's heavier and more trail-oriented than the other bags here. If you're primarily flying between cities, the external frame and outdoor aesthetic may work against you. It won't always pass as a conventional carry-on. Best for: Weekend hikers and campers who need a dependable bag that handles varied terrain without a steep price.

How to Choose the Right 40L Pack

The decision comes down to how you actually travel. If most of your time is spent in transit—airports, trains, city streets—prioritize front-panel access, carry-on compliance, and internal organization. The Nomatic and Tortuga options serve that use case best. If you split your time between trails and cities, the Gregory Facet gives you the suspension system to manage serious loads without looking out of place in an urban setting. Budget travelers who want a proven pack with minimal risk should look at the Osprey Farpoint first. The Peak Design bag is worth the investment only if you're building around its modular system—buying it alone at that price is harder to justify. Whatever you choose, spend time adjusting the fit before you load it fully; even the best pack becomes miserable if the torso length is wrong for your frame.
