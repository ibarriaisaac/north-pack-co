What Makes a Cold-Weather Sleeping Bag

Before diving into specific picks, you need to understand a few key specs that determine whether a bag will actually keep you warm.

Temperature Rating: Bags are rated using the EN/ISO 13537 standard. The comfort rating reflects the temperature at which an average woman can sleep comfortably. The lower limit is for an average man. If you sleep cold, always buy to the comfort rating, not the lower limit. Fill Power and Fill Weight: Higher fill power (e.g., 850 FP vs. 650 FP) means the down is more efficient — it traps more air per ounce. But fill weight (total ounces of down) determines actual warmth. A high-fill-power bag with minimal down won't outperform a heavier bag with more of it. Down vs. Synthetic: Down offers the best warmth-to-weight ratio and compresses well. Synthetic insulation retains warmth when wet and costs less, but it's heavier and bulkier. For truly wet environments, synthetic is the safer choice. Bag Shape: Mummy bags are the warmest because they eliminate dead airspace. Semi-rectangular and quilt designs sacrifice some warmth for comfort and versatility.

Best Overall: Western Mountaineering Alpinlite

The Western Mountaineering Alpinlite Western Mountaineering Alpinlite is one of the most respected sleeping bags in the ultralight backpacking world, and for good reason. Rated to 35°F (comfort), it's technically a 3-season bag — but it earns its place here because its construction quality, 850+ fill power goose down, and weight-to-performance ratio are benchmarks for understanding what a cold-weather bag should do.

At around 1 lb 13 oz, it's featherlight for the warmth it delivers. The draft collar and hood are well-fitted without feeling restrictive, and the YKK zipper glides cleanly even in cold conditions. If you're extending your backpacking season into early winter nights that dip into the mid-30s, this bag handles it. Serious cold-weather mountaineers might want more, but for shoulder-season backpacking, it hits a near-perfect balance.

Best for: Lightweight 3-season and shoulder-season backpacking Limitation: Not enough insulation for true winter camping below 25°F

Best for Extreme Cold: Feathered Friends Snow Bunting EX

When temperatures fall well below zero and the margin for error disappears, the Feathered Friends Snow Bunting EX Feathered Friends Snow Bunting EX is the bag serious mountaineers trust. Rated to -20°F, it uses 900 fill power Polar Select European goose down with a substantial fill weight that doesn't cut corners.

Buying Advice

Match your bag to your actual conditions, not your aspirational ones. A 0°F bag for a trip that bottoms out at 25°F means you'll sleep sweaty and uncomfortable; a 35°F bag for a trip that hits 15°F means you won't sleep at all. Use the EN comfort rating rather than the lower limit, buy one temperature rating colder than your expected low, and prioritize fit — a bag that's too large or too small loses warmth regardless of its specs. For most three-season backpackers extending into cold nights, the REI Magma 15 hits the best balance of performance, weight, and price. For serious winter use, spend what the Feathered Friends Snow Bunting EX costs — it's cheaper than frostbite.
