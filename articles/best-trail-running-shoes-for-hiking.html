Best Trail Running Shoes for Hiking

Trail running shoes have quietly become one of the smartest footwear choices for hikers who cover moderate terrain, move at a faster pace, or simply hate the break-in period that comes with traditional boots. They're lighter, more responsive, and often more comfortable right out of the box. The tradeoff is less ankle support and durability on extremely rocky or technical routes — but for most day hikes and fastpacking trips, they're a genuinely excellent option.

Salomon Speedcross 6

The Salomon Speedcross 6 Salomon Speedcross 6 is one of the most recognizable trail shoes on the market, and for good reason. Its aggressive chevron lug pattern grips mud, wet roots, and loose dirt with confidence that most trail shoes can't match. Hikers who frequent the Pacific Northwest, Appalachian ridgelines, or any region with significant rainfall will immediately notice the difference underfoot.

The Speedcross 6 runs snug through the midfoot and toe box, which reduces foot slippage on descents — a critical feature when you're moving quickly down a steep grade with a loaded pack. The Sensifit lacing system wraps the foot securely without creating pressure points. One honest note: if you have a wider foot, this shoe will feel restrictive. Salomon makes a wide version, but it's not always stocked at every retailer.

The midsole cushioning is moderate — enough for a 10-mile day hike but less forgiving than maximalist options if you're logging back-to-back long days. Durability is excellent, with most users getting 400–500 miles before significant wear.

Best for: Wet conditions, technical rooted trails, hikers who prioritize grip over cushion

---

Hoka Speedgoat 5

If you've been on your feet for eight hours and your joints are starting to send complaints, the Hoka Speedgoat 5 Hoka Speedgoat 5 is the shoe you wish you'd brought. The thick EVA midsole absorbs impact in a way that genuinely changes how your feet and knees feel at mile 15 versus mile 5. This is one of the few trail shoes that fastpackers and thru-hikers have adopted in serious numbers, and the cushion-to-weight ratio is a big reason why.

The outsole uses Vibram Megagrip rubber with a multidirectional lug pattern that performs well on most surfaces — it won't out-grip the Speedcross in deep mud, but it handles rock, dirt, and gravel confidently. The upper on the Speedgoat 5 received meaningful updates from the 4: better breathability, a more secure heel lock, and a slightly wider toe box that accommodates foot swell on long efforts.

Weight sits around 10.6 oz for a men's size 10, which is heavier than some competitors but justified by the protection it offers. The stack height does reduce ground feel, so if you like knowing exactly what's under your foot, this shoe takes some adjustment.

Best for: Long-distance days, fastpacking, hikers with knee or joint sensitivity

---

Brooks Cascadia 17

The Brooks Cascadia 17 Brooks Cascadia 17 is the reliable workhorse in this category — not the flashiest option but consistently excellent across a wide range of conditions. Brooks has refined this model through 17 iterations, and the result is a shoe that feels balanced in almost every category: moderate cushion, dependable grip, solid durability, and a fit that suits a broad range of foot shapes.

The TrailTack rubber outsole is grippy on wet rock and packed dirt without being so aggressive that it feels clunky on pavement sections. The segmented crash pad in the midsole smooths out heel strikes on descents, which matters when you're hiking rather than running — your gait is different and the impact pattern shifts accordingly.

One feature hikers specifically appreciate is the rock plate, which runs through the forefoot and protects against sharp edges without making the shoe feel stiff. The Cascadia 17 also breaks in faster than most trail shoes at this price point. It's a strong first choice for hikers who are new to trail running shoes and want something proven.

Best for: Hikers transitioning from boots, mixed terrain, all-around use

---

Altra Lone Peak 8

The Altra Lone Peak 8 Altra Lone Peak 8 occupies a specific niche that a dedicated group of hikers will not compromise on: zero-drop construction and a wide foot-shaped toe box. If you've dealt with bunions, black toenails, or general discomfort in conventionally shaped shoes, the Lone Peak's design philosophy may genuinely solve problems you've accepted as unavoidable.

Zero-drop means the heel and forefoot sit at the same height — no elevation in the heel the way traditional running or hiking shoes are built. This promotes a more natural foot strike and can reduce calf and Achilles strain once you've adapted to it. The adaptation period is real, though. If you're coming from a heavily cushioned heel-elevated shoe, transition gradually.

The MaxTrac outsole provides competent grip on dirt and gravel. It's not the most aggressive lug pattern in this roundup, so extremely muddy conditions aren't its strength. The cushioning is adequate for moderate days. Where the Lone Peak 8 truly excels is fit: hikers who've never had a shoe feel genuinely comfortable report that this one changes their experience entirely.

Best for: Wide feet, zero-drop advocates, hikers with forefoot or toe issues

---

Merrell Moab Speed 2

Not every hiker needs to spend $145 on trail shoes, and the Merrell Moab Speed 2 Merrell Moab Speed 2 makes a strong argument that you don't have to. Priced $20–$30 below most competitors here, it delivers solid all-around performance without a glaring weakness. The Vibram TC5+ outsole grips well, the fit is roomy enough for most foot widths, and the cushioning is comfortable for day hikes in the 5–12 mile range.

Where you feel the price difference is in the upper materials — slightly less refined breathability than the Speedgoat or Cascadia — and in overall weight. The Moab Speed 2 is not a fast shoe. It's a comfortable, trustworthy shoe for hikers who want the trail running shoe form factor without the trail running shoe price tag. It also crosses over well to casual use, which matters if you want one shoe to handle multiple environments on a trip.

Best for: Budget-conscious hikers, casual and moderate trails, travel versatility

---

Saucony Peregrine 14

The Saucony Peregrine 14 Saucony Peregrine 14 leans toward the performance end of this spectrum. It's light, responsive, and built for hikers who move quickly and want the shoe to move with them. The PWRTRAC outsole is one of the stickier compounds in the trail shoe category, performing particularly well on rocky terrain where purchase matters more than mud-shedding.

The midsole uses PWRRUN foam, which returns energy rather than just absorbing it — you feel a slight spring on the upstroke that makes sustained movement less fatiguing. For hikers who cover ground fast or use trail running shoes because they genuinely want to run sections of a hike, the Peregrine 14 bridges that gap better than anything else in this guide.

The fit is somewhat narrow, similar to the Speedcross, so try before you buy if possible. Cushioning is moderate and the rock plate is thin, making it better suited for faster movement than slow scrambling over boulders.

Best for: Fast hikers, run-hike mixed efforts, rocky technical terrain

How to Choose the Right Trail Running Shoe for Hiking

The right shoe in this category comes down to four variables: terrain type, daily mileage, foot shape, and whether you prioritize cushion or ground feel. If your trails are muddy and rooted, the Speedcross is the easy answer. If you're covering 15+ miles regularly or your knees need protection, start with the Speedgoat. For most hikers doing moderate day trips on varied terrain, the Cascadia 17 is the most forgiving choice with the least risk of a bad fit. Wide-footed hikers should look at the Lone Peak 8 first regardless of other considerations — fit matters more than any other spec on this list, and a properly fitting shoe will always outperform a technically superior shoe that causes discomfort by mile six.
